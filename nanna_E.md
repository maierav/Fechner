## Introduction by the Editor.

Ensouled plants! Does it not sound like a fairy tale?

The animals, and the human as their spokesman, have for so long fancied themselves the masters, the true purpose of creation — for whose nourishment, use, and service the plants exist, indeed for whose sake alone they exist at all. 

Why not, for once, turn the matter around and set the plants upon the throne of the earth? 

If we were plants, and could regard the beings with animal bodies only from the outside, just as humans regard the body of plants — would we not say: 

What do you want, you restless creatures, forever running about, what are you good for but to serve us, the plants, who dwell in noble repose in our ancestral place and need do nothing but stretch out roots and leaves to receive every gift of God as our rightful tribute? 

You live only so that, through your breath, you may prepare the carbonic acid for us, and you die only so that we may draw nitrogen from your decaying bodies. 

You are there to tend us in pots and gardens, field and forest — and in the end we devour you all the same! 

And when we wish, we send our army of bacilli into your blood to torment you. 

We can exterminate you; 

whereas you, however much you may now and then consume in fruits and leaves, 
take from us only a part — or must ever anew see to the spreading of the seeds of our life.



And how many of you are there, anyway? 

Insects, after all, are far more numerous than humans, and yet they too would have to serve us, bearing our pollen as love's messengers from chalice to chalice. 

And much else besides we would say, if we were plants.

And is this inverted world really only a fairy tale? In this form, which lends the plants thought and speech, it is of course the work of a poet. 

But a philosopher took up the idea and examined with care how much truth lies behind the fairy tale. Whoever wishes to know more precisely, let him read this book on the soul-life of the plant, which a fine and acute mind created and first published in the year 1848. In those days the learned world shook its head over it a great deal, and fifty years until the second edition is a long time. But that after fifty years a new edition was needed at all is a sure sign that it was a good book — a book that has a significance that endures, both through itself and through its author. 

And now that it is read again in ever-new editions, the head-shaking in the learned world has grown much less. For the times have changed. 

The fundamental thought, that consciousness in some form floods through the whole of nature, is no longer alien to philosophy; 

it is a consequence of that worldview which sees a twofold shaping of all that happens, as something physical and something psychical. 

Exact research has brought important positive insights concerning the sensory life of plants. The plant-soul is no longer a mere fairy tale; it is in good part a truth that abides. And whatever in Fechner's book may still be fairy tale — well, that, in its own way, abides all the more, for the fairy tale is indeed eternally true — —


Gustav Theodor Fechner was born on April 19, 1801, in Großsärchen (Lower Lusatia), the son of the local pastor; he habilitated in 1823 at the University of Leipzig, where in 1834 he became full professor of physics, and there, after a rich scientific and literary career, he died as an honorary citizen of the city on November 18, 1887. 

A detailed account of his life and work the reader will find in the present writer's study of G. Th. Fechner (Fromann's Classics of Philosophy, Volume I, 2nd ed., Stuttgart 1902).

When Fechner wrote and published the present book on the soul-life of plants, he had passed through a most remarkable crisis in his life. From a severe three-year illness, through which he had seemed irretrievably given over to blindness and mental derangement, he had in the year 1843 recovered — wondrously and beyond all hope. 

His thoughts, which had always readily inclined toward philosophical problems, deepened during his enforced solitude into the life of the soul, and thus the picture of a distinctive conception of the world had gradually risen before his mind's eye in ever-clearer outlines. 

It was an indispensable component of this worldview that the plants, too, share in consciousness and sensation, and this thought he first set forth, in elaborate and engaging fashion, in Nanna.


Already before his illness, in his "Little Book of Life After Death," which had appeared in 1836 under the name Dr. Mises (6th ed. 1906 from Leopold Voß in Hamburg), Fechner had found a way to reconcile his belief in the immortality of the soul with the destruction of the body, by natural law, at the death of the human being. 

When a person dies, the materials that formed their body are not, after all, lost; they merely become components of a larger unity, of the whole body of the earth. 

In similar fashion, Fechner now holds, the psychical elements of the individual life, too, might be preserved in a higher, more general unity; one need only conceive of consciousness as a state belonging not merely to individual humans and animals, but to the whole of nature, everywhere that certain delimitable unities are to be found. 

It would then be the case that, just as the individuals are parts of a higher unity — in the physical sense, parts of the planet — so too their consciousness would at the same time be contained, in some form, within the consciousness of this superordinate unity. 

It lay, therefore, in the interest of the worldview that was taking shape ever more clearly in Fechner, that he should extend the realm of consciousness in nature.

"Nanna" ensouls the plants. Once this thought of the plant-soul had gained definite shape and sound justification, a further step could now be ventured: that the higher unities as well — the planets, of whose total life that of the organisms is a part — be conceived as conscious beings. 

This Fechner set forth in earnest, having earlier done so only jestingly in that book about the angels, some years after the appearance of "Nanna," in his principal philosophical work, "Zend-Avesta, or On the Things of Heaven and the Beyond" (1851, 3rd ed. 1906). 

"Nanna" is the preparation for this.

The ensoulment of the plants already rests upon the fundamental thought that won Fechner the immortal fame of founder of psychophysics and experimental psychology: 

that to every psychical process there corresponds a physical process in the bodily world; 

that the mental is nothing other than the self-appearance of the material, 

while the material is the form in which alone inwardly mental processes can appear to another mental being. 

But for the material processes within a bodily system to set themselves apart, for themselves, as conscious, it is required that they attain a certain degree of intensity — one designated as the threshold. This concept of the threshold, so extraordinarily fruitful for psychophysics, is not yet developed in "Nanna," whereas Fechner introduced it in "Zend-Avesta." For just this reason "Nanna" is of particular interest for the Fechnerian conception of the world. 

One sees clearly how Fechner strives to make plain the thought that the plants possess, to a certain degree, an individual consciousness — one which, however, has its support in the higher unity of a more general consciousness, without on that account losing its individual character. He lacks only, as yet, the sharp, systematic expression for this reciprocal dependence which the concept of the threshold affords.

The time of its appearance, when public interest was concentrated on political questions, was not favorable for "Nanna." 

By the naturalists the book was rejected as fantastical. 

It met with an exceedingly fierce attack from the famous botanist Schleiden; to him Fechner replied in the year 1856 in a separate work, "Professor Schleiden and the Moon," whose first part contains a defense of the views set forth in "Nanna." 

Only later — when, on the one hand, Fechner had regained the confidence of the naturalists through his further works, and, on the other, with the disappearance of speculative nature-philosophy from scientific circles, interest had risen for philosophical questions that rest upon the results of experience — did the Fechnerian idea, too, find a more just appreciation.

The advances of biological research since the appearance of the first edition of "Nanna" would surely, were he still among us, have only confirmed Fechner in his views. 

The discoveries of histology, the more exact knowledge of cell division in plants, of the propagation of stimuli, of the transformation of energy, of the whole life-process in general, then of the interplay of plants and animals, and above all the development of the theory of evolution — all this is well suited to reinforce a conception of plant life favorable to the doctrine of plant-ensoulment. 

One may say that now, too, the chief objection that could be urged on the part of natural science against the existence of a plant-consciousness has been removed by the work of the last ten years. 

In plants one missed precisely those organs to which, in the animal world, the psychical phenomena are bound: 

the sense-instruments for the reception of stimuli of light and pressure, 

the nervous system for the conduction of these stimuli and for the unification of their effects, 

as well as the apparatus for the contraction and movement of the plant's parts. 

But now it has been shown by a number of distinguished researchers that reaction to light, gravity, contact, and other stimuli is of general significance for the plant world; 

in many cases pronounced sense-organs (such as the light-sensitive cells) have been found for this purpose, the structures upon which the conduction of stimuli rests have been revealed, and the mechanism has been demonstrated by which the stimuli are converted into movements of certain parts of the plant. 

It has now been established beyond objection: in the anatomical structure and the physiological functions of plants, as compared with animals, there exists no such difference as would justify denying consciousness to the former on that ground. 

Scientific objections against the ensoulment of plants have become void.

The clearer it becomes that in the manifold of living beings there are indeed stages of development, but no unmediated leaps, the more one must also be convinced that to this physical continuity there corresponds a psychical one. 

Only one must not commit the blunder of wishing to employ the existence of the plant-soul for natural-scientific explanations (compare the editor's book "Souls and Aims," Section V). 

The psychical serves other tasks. Our general picture of the world, the stance of our heart toward the life of the earth as a whole, can be clarified and refined through the recognition of the plant-soul. But no processes whatever in plant life thereby receive an explanation. 

Against such animistic attempts Fechner himself would protest above all — he who always emphasized that the theory of nature may be founded only upon objective law.

That Fechner remained remote from Kant's theory of knowledge proves itself already in "Nanna" to be a hampering circumstance; one need only read his remarks on the concept of the sensory object (pp. 288 f.) to be convinced how powerfully he might have been aided, on this question, by Kant.

Surely Fechner's "Nanna" remains, in the philosophical literature of the nineteenth century, a work as distinctive as it is valuable — one that at the same time deserves to become known in the widest circles as an interesting and stimulating piece of popular reading. 

The present writer therefore gladly undertook the publisher's commission to prepare a second edition, with which, in a manner of speaking, the fiftieth anniversary of the work was reverently celebrated. That this edition is now followed, after only nine years, by the fourth confirms the growth of general interest in Fechner's teachings.

The new edition is, like the second and third, a carefully revised reprint of the first, of the year 1848. Changes have been made only insofar as they concerned printing errors and small details of punctuation, or stylistic and grammatical peculiarities in which Fechner himself was not consistent. They are to be found only where it could be assumed that Fechner would himself have made them in a new edition. For this reason the orthography now in general use could also be introduced. In a few places the use of "was" for "das" and of "als" for "wie," which for our present-day sense of the language made reading more difficult, has been altered. The corrections in the quotations rest upon comparison with the originals. At the close of the book there follows a list of the principal changes. The numbers denote page and line; the original reading stands after the one given in the text, separated by a square bracket.

The page numbers of the first edition are given as a secondary pagination at the head of each page.

## Preface. [By Fechner.]

I confess to having harbored some misgiving about bringing the subject I shall treat in what follows — so wholly dreamlike in appearance, lying in the most peaceful domain of nature — into discussion at a time when the grand urgency and movement of the age claims the attention and interest of everyone, even the otherwise most peaceable, so overwhelmingly, and in regard to matters of so much greater significance. 

For am I not asking that the whispering of the flowers — never heard before in the stillest of times — should now begin to be heard amid the roar of a wind capable of toppling the most anciently rooted trunks; that one should believe in it, attend to it, at a time when the loudest human voice finds it hard to gain a hearing or to hold one? 

And besides, this work has already lain finished and idle for a good while.

Yet I once read how, in certain kinds of deafness, faint voices are heard all the better the louder a drum is beaten at the same time. The shock that deafens a waking ear awakens the sleeping one. 

Now I know well that the drum of the age is not being beaten on behalf of the faint voices of the flowers; but might it not, all the same, prove of service to the hearing of these voices? 

How long was our ear deaf to them — or rather, how long is it since it grew deaf to them; and will it not now be stirred all the more easily by these vanished voices of an early youth, the more strange and new they sound into the surrounding roar, or fade away from it? 

Indeed, am I too bold if I hold it possible that the unknown, soft play that will here unfold may sound to many like a chord struck in anticipation out of a fresh time of youth — one that must one day blossom forth, in will as in deed, from the grave into which, with an already audible rumbling, the old age is sinking?

To this reflection, by which I sought to give myself courage, was added the thought that — now that the first urgency of the time-spirit, grown ill-humored, has if not been appeased then at least slackened somewhat in its tension, and the longer duration of the movement has itself already, here and there, called forth a need for change and for points of rest — a return to quieter interests might also, here and there, appear welcome. 

Will not many, even among those who have gone hard at the bustle in the human world, also be glad for once to seek, for a short while, a point of rest in another world — among beings that nestle, still and contented, at his feet; of whom none presses him, none presses another; and who speak only just so much as he himself wishes to let them speak? 

Into such a world I would lead the reader, and would myself step forth before the little beings and make myself their interpreter, so that — now that all the folk have found their representatives — this little folk, too, may not go without one. Only he to whom it is welcome need follow the invitation.

Perhaps the title-word of this work will be thought far-fetched; in fact, however, it was simply found. Since I wished, for the briefest designation, to set a proper name before it, I wavered for a time between Flora and Hamadryas. The former name seemed to me too botanical after all, the latter somewhat too stiffly antiquarian — and besides bearing only upon the life of trees. 

In the end Flora did stand on the title page, until, in Uhland's Myth of Thor (pp. 147, 152), I came upon the following passage, which seems to me to contain so much that is graceful that I do not deny myself the setting of it down in full, especially since it holds so many a closer point of reference to the content of our work.
