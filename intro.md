Gustav Theodor Fechner (1801-1887) was one of the first experimental psychologists. 

Fechner's claim to fame was the discovery of **_mathematical laws that govern human conscious experience_**. However, Fechner left behind a much larger body of work that united philosophical, scientific, and mathematical thought.

**Several of his books have no recent English translations.** 
**Some have never been translated to English.**

Our goal is to (slowly) change that. For a start, you can find (growing) English translations of 

**[Nanna](nanna_E.md)**

and 

**[Psychophysics: Part 2](PP2_E.md)**

in the sidebar to the left.

You can also find the (modernized) original German texts used for translation.


## Translating Fechner

Fechner's books are in the US public domain, and various scans can be found online. 

So, why not just copy and paste the book into an LMM and have it translated?

1. There is the issue of "Fraktur". The font (typeface) used for some of Fechner's books deviates significantly from the "Roman" (Antiqua) fonts that we are used to. Standard OCR software that transcribes text from scanned images struggles with Fraktur, where the letters "s", "f", and "k" can look alike. 

2. There is the issue of outdated German. Fechner's writing deviates both from modern written and spoken German. Not to the degree that it cannot be understood by a contemporary German, but to the degree that an LMM trained on modern German can make mistakes.

So, how are we dealing with all that?

1. We carefully (word-by-word) parse OCR from the original texts to fix mistakes. Any, problems, such as transcribing "seiner" (his) as "feiner" (finer) are fixed.

2. We ever-so-slightly updated the German before translation to English. Most of these edits are simply adjustments of spelling to modern convention, but occasionally we also adjusted minor grammatical choices that sound "odd", or "wrong" to modern ears.

3. We use LMM's for initial translation. Then edit the output to a single, subjective, human bi-lingual judgement's satisfaction - flawed as it might be.

## Formatting

The original books are walls of text. So are many of the PDF copies of these books, including the few existing English translations.

For better or for worse, today's reaction to such texts is: _tl;dr_ (too long; didn't read).

Fair. At least to the fact that we can do better these days in terms of visual presentations of text.

So, we took some liberty with respect to line breaks, paragraphs, typeface setting, and other aesthetic choices that might make for easier reading. We also took liberty with adding (hyperlinked) context. And we added a few, AI-generated illustrations to break up the monotony.

The basic text is just a markup-document, so anyone is free to download a personal copy to get rid of all superfluous elements.
