Gustav Theodor Fechner (1801-1887) was one of the first experimental psychologists. 

For some reason, Fechner is not quite as widely known as one might expect for a founder of a scientific field. It seems, however, that his influence is slowly growing.

Fechner's claim to fame was the discovery of mathematical laws that govern human conscious experience.

Yet, Fechner left behind a much larger body of work that united philosophical, scientific, and mathematical thought.

Several of these books have no recent English translations. Some have never been translated to English.

Our goal is to (slowly) change that.

## Translating Fechner

Fechner's books are in the US public domain, and various scans can be found online. 

So, why not just copy and paste the book into an LMM and have it translated?

First, there is the issue of "Fraktur". The font (typeface) used for most of Fechner's books deviates significantly from the "Roman" (Antiqua) fonts that we are used to. The reason is a bit of a quirk of history, but standard OCR software that transcribes text from scanned text tends to struggle with Fraktur, where the letters "s", "f", and "k" often look alike. 

Second, there is the outdated German. FEchner's writing deviates significantly both from modern written and spoken German. Not to the degree that it cannot be understood by a contemporary German, but to the degree that an LMM trained on modern German can manke mistakes.

So, how are we dealing with all that?

First, we carefully (word-by-word) parse OCR from the original texts to fix mistakes. While rare, the problem of automated transcirption sometimes replacing the original "seiner" (his) with "feiner" (fine) can cause semantic confusion, if overlooked.

Second, we ever-so-slightly update the German before translation to English. Most of these edits are simply adjusting spelling to modern convention, but occassionally we also adjusted minor grammatical choices that sound "odd", or even "wrong" to modern ears.

Third, we will use LMM's for initial translation indeed. And then edit the output to a single, subjective, human bi-lingual judgement, flawed as it might be.

## Formatting

The original books are walls of text. 

So are many of the PDF copies of these books, including the few existing English translations.

For better or for worse, today's reaction to such texts is: tl;dr (too long; didn't read).

Fair. 

At least to the fact that we can do better these days in terms of visual presentations of text.

So, we took great liberty here with respect to line breaks, paragraphs, typeface setting, and other aesthetic choices that might make for easier reading.

We also took liberty with adding (hyperlinked) context.

And added a few, AI-generated illustrations to break up the monotony.

All these choices are questionable. The text is fundamentally just a markup-document, so anyone is free to download a personal copy and get rid of these superfluos elements.

## Timeline

There is no timeline. This is a hobby/passion-project, with updates occuring whenever they may. 

We will provide a coarse translation first to cut down on the suspense, while also combing with a fine-toothed comb from start-to-finish.
