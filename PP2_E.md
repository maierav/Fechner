## [English] Psychophysics - Volume 2

## PREFACE TO THE SECOND VOLUME.

In addition to the general preliminary remarks in the preface to the first volume, I find it useful to add some orienting remarks about the content of this second volume in particular. 

It contains, apart from a historical chapter in which I have recorded the precedents, the origin, and the course of these investigations, some additions, and a glossary of the new or specifically defined terms used in this document, in three main sections:

1) Formulas and conclusions of the psychic measure;
2) Special investigations on some sensory domains;
3) Inner psychophysics. 

The first of these sections essentially contains only the mathematical presentation and connection of what is presented in terms of laws and facts in the first part. Consequently, it will not offer any new substantive content to the physiologist and psychologist. Also, after looking at the mass of formulas contained in this section, one will probably ask what is actually gained from it. In this regard, I have briefly explained one of the main formulas, the measurement formula, on page XX, and for the other formulas, I have not failed to point out the applications they promise or already provide. For example, the distribution formulas of sensation in Chapter 21 give rise to some interesting conclusions, the application of the difference measurement formula to the estimation of star magnitudes, and the shape of a location in the assessment of the constancy of errors are discussed in specific chapters (25 and 27). The solution that has been provided in Chapter 30, pages XXX ff., to the puzzle of the octave in music theory may perhaps claim special interest.

The main interest that is currently associated with these formulas remains theoretical, as they provide a previously missing measure not only for simple sensations but also for representing their functional relationships when applied. The principles of dealing with this subject matter are much more important than the formulas, which are only specific applications of the principles. The principles, as presented in Chapters 6, 7, 18, 22, 30, 31, and 32, should be understandable even to those not well-versed in mathematics, and the durability of the teachings presented in this work relies on the durability of these principles. As for the formulas, they may undergo various modifications. It should be emphasized that as they are presented here, they are merely approximations, especially when applied in the field of outer psychophysics, as I explained earlier in this section's introduction. These formulas may require different modifications or corrections for various sensory domains and different modes of sensory application. Even if these modifications were more securely established than they are at present, they could not be accommodated in the general treatment of the subject matter here, not only because they would need to be different for different domains *) but also because they would undoubtedly be irrelevant to inner psychophysics. However, I believe that these formulas provided here will remain important for outer and, thus, experimental psychophysics. They will serve as the foundation for further development and precise establishment of the mathematical aspects of the theory of sensation, as evidenced by recent progress in the field of light sensation, which I have mentioned in the addenda. 

One may criticize my practice of assigning special names to the most important formulas as an ambitious play. Indeed, if every formula in all mathematical investigations were to be given a special name, mathematics would soon compete with botany and zoology in the richness of its nomenclature. However, given the numerous references I had to make to the main formulas, both in this work and potentially in the future if the theory presented here gains acceptance, the advantage gained in terms of brevity and the avoidance of extensive cross-referencing will likely be appreciated.

The second volume might easily raise questions about why some topics are treated in such detail while many others that could equally deserve attention in psychophysics are entirely omitted. My answer is simple: I have sought to thoroughly address the subjects that could shed new light from the perspective of the theory presented here or whose treatment effectively integrated into the general framework of this theory. This work, explicitly, has more the character of an investigation than a textbook. Additionally, I believed that it would not be appreciated if readers found chapters on physiology and physics, which are covered elsewhere, repeated here. Undoubtedly, from a certain perspective, the entire field of neurology and sensory studies can be drawn into psychophysics. In the future, as physiology and physics continue to expand, they may gladly leave certain border areas to a self-constituted psychophysics. However, it will always be better for this discipline to rely on and complement those fields rather than repeating them.

In the presentation of some tactile experiment series in the second section, I have preemptively provided further clarification on certain points related to what was discussed in the first volume regarding the method of the mean error. I will have to wait and see if the public shows sufficient interest in the entire scope of these investigations before I can publish the “measurement methods" that I have referred to earlier in terms of the details of the methods and still have many aspects to address.

In the third section, one would search in vain for a complete and comprehensive system of inner psychophysics; entire major areas that should belong to it are missing. Primarily, the goal was to establish general perspectives for internal psychophysics and to gain initial entry points from which research with increasing confidence in the results could be conducted. If I am not mistaken, those chapters that are at the forefront of inner psychophysics (Chapters 36, 37, 38, 39) reflect this character, and here too, I place the main emphasis on the principles. I have gradually omitted more and more of the details in my explanations, and I still worry that I may have provided too much rather than too little. However, the subject had to be tackled to demonstrate that it is approachable, even if some points of attack may be replaced in the future by more suitable ones or some attacks by more skillful or compelling ones. From this perspective, I ask you to consider the few explanations of inner psychophysics. Thus, the schematic representation of some of the most general and important psychophysical relationships, which I have particularly used in Chapters 42 and 45, may be seen as a framework from one point of view and as a surrogate from another. I consider this representation useful, indeed very useful, given the existing gap in this regard. However, this framework must eventually be filled with specifics, and the surrogate must be replaced by more direct representations that it currently represents.

For those whose primary interest is empirical, this volume provides some new experimental material primarily in Chapters 34, 35, and 44. The observations on contrast ratios, which were referred to in Volume II, pg. XXX, could not be included here, partly because they were not fully edited and partly because they had expanded too much in scope. However, they were published almost simultaneously with this volume in the Reports of the Saxon Society in 1860 under the title "On the Sensation of Contrast." I also report on the unfortunately unsuccessful subsequent execution of an important acoustic experiment that was mentioned in Volume II, pg. XXX in the addenda. Additionally, the addenda contain references to some more recent and significant research by Helmholtz.

If one wishes to do so, one can consider a forthcoming, small, and written for a broad audience work titled "On the Question of the Soul" as a supplement to this manuscript. This work partly summarizes and partly expands on the content of some earlier writings. It deals with the prospects briefly hinted at in Chapter 45 of the current manuscript, which open up from the perspective of a most general formulation of psychophysics into the realm of religion and natural philosophy. The viewpoints presented in this work, without claiming an exactness in form and substance that has not yet been reached, should nevertheless come close to exactness, considering the nature of the tasks and our means of knowledge. I have attempted to formulate them more precisely under the name "Principles of Empirical Belief." While the views summarized there have not enjoyed much popularity due to their contradiction of the prevailing common, theological, and philosophical worldview, and they may not hope for immediate acceptance, it is easy to see from the discussions in Chapters 45 and 46 of this manuscript that they are only a precursor to the ultimate goal of psychophysics developing on the principles of this manuscript. Psychophysics cannot become universal without expanding, deepening, and elevating the intellectual structure of the world beyond the currently accepted boundaries. I say this with the conviction that the resistance of the times to such views will also affect the reception of this psychophysics, which inherently contains them as consequences. However, I am equally convinced that this resistance will ultimately fail against the solid foundation and future development of this doctrine.
Leipzig, August 18, 1860.
